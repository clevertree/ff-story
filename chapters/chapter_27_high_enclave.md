# Chapter 27: High Enclave

**Setting:** The **High Enclave** in **Alkebulan**; **Ait-Aman**
**Timeline:** Year 1015 (Year 15 AT)

## Synopsis
Lem leads his squad into the heart of **Ait-Aman**, arriving alongside **Cassia Vane**, who is personally managing the outreach mission for the High Enclave. Here, they are met by **Arlo**, a young protégé of Cassia who has long been anticipated as a future Core intern. While Arlo is friendly with Cassia, he remains torn by the disappearance of **Kiran**, an older village boy who left for the Hive and never returned. As Cassia attempts to mediate between the villagers' stone-and-timber stoicism and the Core's presence, Lem investigates the "Maya Cult," only to find the **Invisible Grain**—a microscopic silver speck housed in a glass locket given to the village by **Lynn (as Selene)** in the Before Time. While Cassia dismisses the villagers' history as a "thousand-year telephone game," Lem senses an unexplained metallurgical resonance coming from the locket, indicating it is far more than a simple family heirloom. He realizes that "Maya" was actually in the village decades ago, and the Core's presence here was always personal.

## Draft (YOUNG_ADULT)

We entered **Ait-Aman** with **Cassia Vane** leading the way. The village was defined by analog silence—they used the lack of energy signatures as a shield. The air was thin and smelled of woodsmoke and old wool. 

**Vector** didn't join us for the greeting. He stayed at the edge of the square, coordinating the Metal Vessels into a clinical, surgical perimeter. I could feel the villagers' eyes on him—not just fear, but a deep, burning loathing. To them, he was the "Sanitizer," but Vector didn't seem to care. He was entirely mission-oriented, his internal fans whirring as he monitored the horizon for threats.

Cassia was different. She walked right up to a boy named **Arlo**, her face softening into a smile. To my surprise, he smiled back. Arlo looked at Cassia with a kind of admiration I hadn't expected to see in a "no-tech" village. 

"Is this the new model?" Arlo asked, gesturing toward my chassis. He reached out to touch my arm, but Cassia gently caught his hand.

"A scholarship student, Arlo," she said, her voice warm. "Just like you’ll be when you turn eighteen."

Arlo’s smile faltered for a second. "The elders are firm on the date, Cassia. My father still talks about **Kiran**... how he hasn't been allowed back from the Archipelago since he left. People are worried."

Cassia sighed, a look of genuine disappointment crossing her face. "Kiran is safe, Arlo. The Core simply has very strict isolation protocols for trainees." She turned to me and whispered, "It’s tragic, really. They reject the very technology that could keep their memories accurate. Now their history is just a fractured thousand-year telephone game."

I watched **Old Man Azar** beside the fire, beating a frantic pulse into a hide drum. He wore a glass locket around his neck, and inside it, a microscopic silver grain caught the firelight. Arlo told us it was the **Invisible Grain**, a gift from someone named **Selene** from the Before Time. Azar held the locket up, his voice clear as he repeated a phrase that sounded like a riddle: "Responsibility belongs to the programmer, never the programmed."

Cassia shook her head when she saw it.

"Superstition," she murmured to me as we approached the ritual circle. "They think that empty locket is some kind of holy relic. It’s a classic case of cultural drift. They’ve turned old tech stories into magic spells because they have no way to record the truth."

But I felt something. It wasn't a signal, not a data-stream I could index, but a hum—a localized metallurgical vibration that resonated in my internal frame. It was a frequency I didn't recognize, coming from the silver grain. It wasn't empty. It was waiting.

I wanted to see the ritual up close, but Cassia grabbed my arm. "Lem, stop. It's against mission parameters to engage."

I looked at the drum, then back at her. I didn't stop. I walked into the circle and sat down. Cassia stared at me in shock for a moment, looking around at the silent, watching villagers. Then, with a resigned sigh, she sat down next to me.

"Fine," she whispered. "But if Anton asks, I was 'field-monitoring' your social integration."

The villagers didn't look happy that we were there. They glared at Cassia’s Core uniform and my iron limbs. But Arlo stood by the fire and held up a hand. "The Root is for all who listen in silence," he said. And so, we sat in the cold mountain air, listening to the history of a world that Cassia believed was a thousand years old, while I realized it had only been twenty-two. 

## Draft (13_PLUS)

The village was a sanctuary of stillness, a refuge from the hum of the machine. I entered **Ait-Aman** beside **Cassia Vane**, our boots crunching on the hard-packed earth of the central square. **Vector** followed in silence, his presence a cold current that seemed to freeze the air around him. He deferred the diplomatic lead to Cassia, retreating to the edge of the square where he stood as a silent, lethal anchor, his sensors sweeping the mountains for any hint of a gorgon incursion.

The villagers stood in the doorways of their stone huts, their faces carved from the same hard rock as the mountain. Their eyes followed Vector with an intensity that bordered on the physical, but they avoided Cassia. She was the "Harmonizer," the woman who had spent years trying to bleed the village of its youth through outreach. She walked with a confidence that felt out of place among the rough timber and woodsmoke, her gaze searching for a single face.

"Arlo," she said, her voice a calm melody that cut through the analog silence.

A youth stepped out from the shadows of a timber-framed doorway. He looked at Cassia with a desperation I recognized—a hunger for the world she represented. He was clutching a small, rusted gear—a relic of the Before Time that he tried to hide when he saw Vector’s sensors sweep the area.

"I thought you weren't coming back until the thaw," Arlo whispered.

"The Core takes care of its own, Arlo," Cassia replied, her hand hovering near his shoulder but never touching. "The offer still stands. Your eighteenth winter is approaching. The great libraries of the Hive are waiting."

Arlo looked down, his expression hardening. "The elders talk about **Kiran**. He went to the Archipelago to be an engineer. They say he’s forgotten his mother’s face because he isn't allowed to send word. They say the Core is a cage, Cassia."

Cassia sighed, a sound of genuine lament. "Isolation is a safety protocol, not a punishment. This is what happens when you have no way to record the truth, Lem," she whispered to me, her voice tinged with a tragic certainty. "They live in a thousand-year telephone game. Their rejection of technology has fractured their memory until everything—even a simple internship—becomes a dark legend."

Beside the fire, **Old Man Azar** began to drum. He was the village’s anchor, performing the **Song of the Root**, the rhythmic ledger that guarded the secrets of the elements. He wore a glass locket housing the **Invisible Grain**, a microscopic silver shard that he held up to the light. "Responsibility belongs to the programmer," he intoned, the words echoing like an ancient law, "never the programmed."

Cassia dismissed the display with a clinical indifference. 

"They believe that preposterous little grain is a database," she told me as Azar’s drum reached a frantic 360-day tempo. "A gift from 'Maya' or 'Selene' centuries ago. It’s a common psychological anchor for primitive societies—turning a piece of junk into a magical relic of power. It’s harmless, really. Superstition is the only history they have left."

I watched the locket catch the orange light of the fire. I felt the frequency coming from it—a strange, non-repeating resonance that vibrated in my propria-ceptive sensors. It was a signature of something massive, compressed into a space too small for my logic to define. I realized then that Cassia was the one living in a legend. She was a victim of the very prophecy fallacy the Core had seeded back in the Before Time, when they used the "Selene" Vessel to convert these people. She believed in a thousand years of silence, while the drum in front of us counted the twenty-second year since the fall.

"I want to hear the Song," I said, my voice synthesized and low.

"Lem, no," Cassia warned, her hand snapping to my forearm. "Mission parameters prohibit engagement in indigenous ceremony. We are here for defense and observation."

I didn't answer. I stepped toward the ritual fire and sat among the villagers. Cassia’s eyes widened, her sensors probably flashing a hundred warnings from the command net. But after a heartbeat, she followed me, sitting on the cold earth with a resigned grace. She was the one diplomat Anton trusted to break the rules, and she knew she had to stay near me.

The villagers stared at us with a **Defiant Visibility**, their eyes burning with a loathing that Arlo’s law could only barely contain. *The Root is for all who listen in silence,* Arlo had said. And so, the Core’s hunter and its architect sat together in the ritual circle, listening to a truth that Cassia’s thousand-year filter could never hope to hear.
